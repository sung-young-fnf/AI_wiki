Because “similarity” is not same as “relevance”

For professionals in high-stakes industries (like Pharmacovigilance), ‘close enough’ is a dangerous standard for AI. Traditional vector-based RAG often fails when faced with dense, multi-layered documents because it lacks a sense of context and hierarchy. It treats a document like a bag of words rather than a structured map of knowledge.

In this article, I am explaining a new approach by Mingtian Zhang and Yu Tang who have created “PageIndex”. Its a a reasoning-based retrieval system that replaces vector similarity search with LLM reasoning over document structure. Their system achieved 98.7% accuracy on the FinanceBench benchmark, and as I’ll show, it dramatically outperforms traditional RAG on pharmacovigilance documents.

But lets first look at the problem with traditional RAGs.

Why do we need RAG?

LLMs have a context window (a maximum number of tokens they can process at once). A 200-page PBRER might have 80,000+ tokens, but even if the LLM can handle that many, performance degrades as context grows (a phenomenon called “context rot”. The LLM starts missing details buried in the middle of long inputs.

So, the question becomes: How do we get the LLM to answer questions about documents that are too long to fit (or perform well) in its context window? The answer is: Don’t feed the whole document. Feed only the relevant parts. That is what RAG (Retrieval Augmented Generation) does.

Traditional Vector RAG

Let’s use an example. Say you have a 25-page PBRER for Cardiozan (a fictional oral anticoagulant), and a safety assessor asks:

“What risk minimization measures were taken for hepatotoxicity?”

(I have uploaded the document on the Github repo)

Traditional vector RAG will do the following:

Step 1: Chunk the document into fixed-size pieces

The PBRER gets split into, say, 17 chunks of ~300 words each. The chunking doesn’t care about section boundaries. It just counts words and cuts:

Chunk 1: [Title page … start of Introduction]
Chunk 2: [… rest of Introduction … start of Marketing Status]
Chunk 3: [… Marketing Status … start of Safety Actions]
…
Chunk 9: [… middle of SAE table, row cut in half …]
Chunk 15: [… end of Benefit-Risk … start of Conclusions]

If you notice, Section 3 (Safety Actions) might get split across chunks 3 and The table of identified risks in Section 8 may be probably cut in half.

Step 2: Embed each chunk into a vector

Each chunk gets converted into a list of numbers (typically 384 or 1536 dimensions) using an embedding model. These numbers represent the “meaning” of the chunk in mathematical space.

Chunk 3 → [0.023, -0.156, 0.891, …, 0.044] (384 numbers)
Chunk 9 → [0.112, -0.034, 0.567, …, -0.089] (384 numbers)
Step 3: Store all vectors in a vector database

All 17 vectors go into a database like Pinecone, ChromaDB, or Weaviate.

Step 4: Embed the user’s question

The assessor’s question gets converted to a vector using the same model:

“What risk minimization measures were taken for hepatotoxicity?”
→ [0.045, -0.178, 0.734, …, 0.012]

Step 5: Find the most “similar” chunks

The system calculates the cosine similarity between the question vector and every chunk vector, then returns the top 3 closest matches.

Step 6: Feed those chunks + question to the LLM

The LLM reads only those 3 chunks and generates an answer.

A PV professional reading this PBRER would naturally flip between these sections, following cross-references like “see Section 7.1” and “refer to Appendix B.” They understand the document’s hierarchy. However a vector RAG can not do that.

The Five Fundamental Problems

After testing multiple queries, I identified five systematic problems with vector RAG on PV documents:

Similarity ≠ Relevance

A chunk that says “risk minimization measures are described in Section 3” is highly similar to a question about risk minimization. But it’s not relevant. It’s a pointer but not the answer. Vector search can’t tell the difference.

Chunking Destroys Document Structure

PBRERs follow a mandated ICH E2C(R2) structure. Every section has regulatory significance. When you chunk by word count, you break:

Tables (rows split across chunks)

Section boundaries (headers separated from content)

Cross-references (the reference and the target land in different chunks)

Cross-References Are Invisible

PV documents are full of internal references: “see Section 7.1,” “refer to Appendix A,” “as described in the CCDS changes (Section 4).” A human reader follows these naturally. Vector search treats each chunk in isolation. It has no mechanism to “follow” a reference.

Medical Vocabulary Mismatch

Consider this query:

“Has the drug been shown to affect heart rhythm?”

The answer is on page 15. The QT Prolongation signal evaluation. But the document uses terms like “QT prolongation,” “QTcF interval,” “Torsade de Pointes,” and “ddQTcF.” It never says “heart rhythm.”

A PV professional knows that “heart rhythm” = “cardiac electrophysiology” = “QT/QTc assessment.” Embedding models don’t have that domain knowledge. The vector for “heart rhythm” is far from the vector for “QT prolongation” in embedding space.

Now, there’s a subtlety here: if a summary or conclusions page happens to mention “QT prolongation,” the LLM generating the answer can bridge the gap. It understands the medical relationship. But this means the system is relying on luck (did the conclusions page land in the top-3 chunks?) rather than understanding. And crucially, it finds the summary rather than the source data. In a regulatory context, the conclusion without the evidence is incomplete.

No Iterative Reasoning

Some questions need information from multiple sections. “What is the incidence of DILI and what are the key risk factors?” requires data from:

Section 6.3 (literature incidence — Yamamoto study: 0.42%)
Section 7.1 (spontaneous reporting rate: 0.38/1,000 PY, risk factors listed)
Section 8.1 (consolidated risk characterization)

Vector RAG does a single retrieval pass,it gets 3 chunks and that’s it. There’s no ability to say, “I found the incidence, but I still need risk factors. Let me look in another section.”

PageIndex: Reasoning-Based Retrieval

PageIndex takes a fundamentally different approach. Instead of

Chunk → Embed → Search by similarity

It does:

Build a tree index → LLM reads the tree → LLM reasons about where to look → Fetches actual content → Repeats if needed

So there are no vectors and embeddings. No similarity scores. Just an LLM reading a map and thinking about where to go.

How PageIndex works

Instead of chunking the document into equal pieces, PageIndex builds a hierarchical tree that mirrors the document’s actual structure:

PBRER for Cardiozan
├── [N001] Title Page & TOC (pages 1–2)
├── [N002] Introduction (page 3)
│ Summary: Factor Xa inhibitor, NVAF/DVT/PE indications, 10–20mg…
├── [N004] Safety Actions (page 5)
│ Summary: EMA SmPC update, FDA REMS modification, DHPC,
│ RMP v8.0, study CZ-HEP-501 initiated…
├── [N008] Signal Evaluation (pages 13–15)
│ ├── [N008a] Hepatotoxicity - CONFIRMED signal (page 13)
│ │ Summary: PRR 2.8, 198 new cases, 12 fatal, RUCAM scores,
│ │ dechallenge+ 83.6%, rechallenge+ 72.7%…
│ ├── [N008b] Thrombocytopenia - ONGOING (page 14)
│ │ Summary: 187 new cases, anti-PF4 antibodies, HIT-like…
│ └── [N008c] QT Prolongation - CLOSED/Refuted (page 15)
│ Summary: TQT study ddQTcF +3.2ms (<10ms threshold),
│ confounded by amiodarone/sotalol…
├── [N009] Risk Characterization (pages 16–17)
│ Summary: Hepatotoxicity NOW IDENTIFIED RISK, incidence 0.38/1000 PY…
└── [N013] Appendix A: ICSR Line Listings (page 23)
 Summary: Fatal hepatic cases with RUCAM scores…

Each node has a summary packed with key facts which are enough for an LLM to decide “is this where my answer might be?” without reading the full content.

When the user asks a question, the LLM enters a reasoning loop:

READ the tree index (already in context)
REASON: “Based on the summaries, which node(s) might have my answer?”
FETCH: Request the actual content of that node
EXTRACT: Pull relevant information from the content
DECIDE: Do I have enough? → Answer. Need more? → Go to step 2.

So, LLM is doing the retrieval, not a similarity algorithm.

Query 1: “What is the reporting rate for serious adverse events?”

The answer is a single sentence on page 9: “The reporting rate for serious cases was 7.1 per 1,000 patient-years.”

VectorRAG was not able to answer it and returned pages 7–8 (exposure tables with “patient-years” repeated in every row), page 12 (literature review mentioning “incidence” rates), and page 25 (literature search strategy containing the keywords “adverse” and “safety” as search terms).

The actual answer (one sentence on page 9) was outranked because it mentions “reporting rate” only once, while the exposure tables repeat “patient-years” and “rate” dozens of times across rows of data. The embedding model saw more word overlap in the tables and ranked them higher.

PageIndex was able to retrieve the correct answer.

This shows that even “easy” lookups can fail when data-heavy pages drown out the answer page in similarity scores. The answer is a needle in the haystack of the SAE tables and exposure data.

Appendix A lists three detailed case narratives on page 23. When the document was chunked into 300-word pieces, the chunk boundary cut between case 1 and case 2. The chunk containing case CZ-2023–00891 (RUCAM 9, Hy’s Law, the strongest causality evidence) also contained conclusions text that diluted its similarity score. So it ranked below the top-3 cutoff.

In a signal evaluation, this could affect the classification of the risk. That’s not a minor omission. It’s the difference between “probable” and “highly probable” drug causation.

In this query, both approaches produced correct and comprehensive answers. Vector RAG retrieved page 17 (Potential Risks / Missing Information), which contains a compact summary list: “CCDS updated, DHPC distributed, post-marketing study initiated, educational materials distributed.” It also caught pages 6–7 (CCDS changes) in an adjacent chunk. So it assembled a good answer from a summary page plus one source section.

PageIndex navigated deliberately to the three primary sections: N004 (Safety Actions, page 5) → N005 (CCDS Changes, page 6) → N009a (Risk Characterization, page 16). Its answer has specific regulatory dates (June 15, Aug 22, Oct 2023), the CYP3A4 interaction warning (+160% exposure increase), and clear categorization.

However there is a subtle difference: Vector RAG’s answer is built from a summary that lists the measures. PageIndex’s answer is built from the sections that describe the measures. Both answers read well, but if an assessor needed to verify the details when exactly was the REMS modified? What was the ALT threshold? PageIndex points to the authoritative source sections, while Vector RAG points to a summary reference.

With VectorRAG, when the document happens to contain a convenient summary of the answer on a single page, and that page happens to land in a top-3 chunk, the system works. The problem is that you can’t predict when that will happen.

In a regulatory context, “the signal was refuted” is a conclusion. An assessor needs the evidence (the study design, the numbers, the confounders). The conclusion without supporting data doesn’t meet the standard for a safety evaluation. in this query, Vector RAG found the table of contents while PageIndex found the source which is more relevant.

Why this matters for Pharmacovigilance

Pharmacovigilance is a highly regulated domain. In this context of document intelligence, I believe below are some specific implications:

Signal Detection and Assessment

Signal evaluation requires pulling data from multiple PBRER sections such as case series data (Section 6), disproportionality analysis (Section 7), literature (Section 6.3), risk characterization (Section 8). A vector RAG system helping with signal reviews will miss pieces. A reasoning-based system can follow the document’s own structure, just like you would.

Regulatory Inspections and Audit Trails

EU GVP Module VI requires traceability. You need to show why you retrieved certain information for a safety decision. Vector RAG’s explanation: “cosine similarity = 0.82.” can’t be used in audits or inspections.

PageIndex produces a full reasoning trace:

Step 1: Read tree index. Question about risk minimization measures. Step 2: Node N004 (Safety Actions): summary mentions EMA, FDA, HCP actions. FETCH. Step 3: Found regulatory actions. Cross-reference says “see Section 7.1.” Need more. Step 4: Node N009a (Risk Characterization): summary mentions consolidated measures. FETCH. Step 5: Have complete picture. ANSWER.

That trace is your audit trail.

MedDRA Terminology

The same adverse event appears under different Preferred Terms, High Level Terms, and System Organ Classes in MedDRA. “Thrombocytopenia” might be coded under “Blood and lymphatic system disorders” in one section and discussed as “immune-mediated platelet destruction” in the literature review. Vector embeddings treat these as unrelated strings. An LLM understands they’re the same thing.

Cross-Document References

PBRERs don’t exist in isolation. They reference the Reference Safety Information (RSI), the Risk Management Plan (RMP), the Company Core Data Sheet (CCDS), clinical study reports, and previous PBRERs. A reasoning-based system can, in principle, navigate across documents, something vector RAG fundamentally cannot do.

ICH-Mandated Structure

PBRERs, PSURs, DSURs: these all follow standardized ICH formats with predictable section hierarchies. This is a gift for reasoning-based retrieval. The tree index maps directly to ICH section numbering. Vector RAG ignores this structure entirely by chunking the document into equal-sized pieces.
